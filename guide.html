How to Fix a Leaky Faucet

⏱️ 30–60 minutes 💰 $8–$25 in parts 🟢 Beginner friendly ⭐ 4.9 (182 ratings)

A dripping faucet wastes up to 3,000 gallons of water per year and typically costs $150–$300 to fix professionally. Most leaks are caused by a worn washer or O-ring — parts that cost under $5. This guide walks you through diagnosing and fixing the most common faucet types.

Turn off the water supply
Find the shut-off valve under the sink — turn it clockwise until it stops. If there's no valve under the sink, turn off the main water supply. Open the faucet to release any remaining pressure and drain the lines.
⚠️ Safety first: Never skip this step. Working on a live water line will soak you and your cabinet instantly.
✓
Identify your faucet type
The fix varies by faucet type. Check which you have: Ball faucet (one handle that rotates), Cartridge faucet (one or two handles, pulls up/down), Ceramic disc faucet (wide cylindrical body, single lever), or Compression faucet (two separate handles, older style). If unsure, search your faucet's brand + model on the manufacturer's site.
💡 Tip: Take a photo of your faucet before disassembling. It'll help if you need to search for parts or reassemble later.
✓
Remove the handle
Look for a decorative cap on top of the handle (often pops off with a flathead screwdriver). Underneath is a screw — remove it with a Phillips head. Gently wiggle the handle off. Some handles need a handle puller tool if they're corroded on.
✓
Locate and remove the cartridge or stem
Use an adjustable wrench or pliers (wrap jaws with tape to protect chrome) to unscrew the packing nut. Pull out the stem or cartridge. Note the orientation before removing — take a photo so you know which way it goes back in.
💡 Tip: Bring the old cartridge to your hardware store. They can match it exactly and save you from buying the wrong replacement.
✓
Replace the washer, O-ring, or cartridge
For compression faucets: the rubber washer at the bottom of the stem is almost always the culprit. Remove the screw holding it and swap in the new washer. For cartridge faucets: replace the entire cartridge. For ball faucets: replace the seats, springs, and O-rings (a repair kit has everything). Coat new rubber parts lightly with plumber's grease before installing.
✓
Reassemble and test
Reverse your disassembly steps — seat, packing nut, handle, screw, cap. Don't overtighten; snug is enough. Slowly turn the water supply back on (counterclockwise). Turn the faucet on and off a few times. Check for drips at the spout and leaks around the base of the handle. If it still drips, the seat itself may be damaged — a seat wrench can resurface it.
💡 Tip: Turn the water on slowly — sudden pressure can cause leaks at connections you didn't touch.

Use a repair kit, not individual parts

For ball faucets especially, buy a complete repair kit for your brand ($8–$15). It includes all the parts that wear out together, and it's much cheaper than replacing them individually.

Plumber's grease is non-negotiable

Silicone-based plumber's grease ($4) extends the life of rubber O-rings and washers dramatically. Apply a thin coat to every rubber part before reassembling.

Know when to call a plumber

If you see corrosion on the valve seat, cracks in the faucet body, or water leaking from inside the wall — stop and call a pro. These indicate bigger problems that DIY won't fix.

What if I can't find my faucet's cartridge at the hardware store?

Search the brand + model number online — most manufacturers sell replacement parts directly. Moen, Delta, and Kohler all have excellent parts programs. Moen even has a lifetime warranty on many cartridges and will ship you one free.

Can I fix a dripping shower head the same way?

The principle is the same, but shower valves are more complex. Check our Shower Valve Repair guide for a separate walkthrough.

The leak is under the sink, not the spout. Is this the right guide?

No — that's likely a supply line or drain connection issue. See our Fix an Under-Sink Leak guide.
